# Institutional Thematic Research: Grid-Scale AI Power Stack

## A. Cover
- Theme: Grid-Scale AI Power Stack
- Date: 2026-02-07
- Investment call: Multi-year power bottlenecks create durable pricing power for selected generation, interconnect, and thermal-management suppliers.

## B. Executive Summary
- Structural shift:
  - AI compute demand is now constrained by power availability and cooling throughput, not only chip supply.
  - Value capture is moving toward physical bottlenecks: transmission equipment, switchgear, backup power, and liquid cooling.
- Why now:
  - Utility interconnection queues and permitting timelines are lengthening while hyperscaler capex remains elevated.
  - The market is still pricing this as a short-cycle hardware story, not a medium-cycle infrastructure buildout.
- Winners / losers:
  - Winners: transmission grid suppliers, high-voltage component makers, thermal management specialists.
  - Losers: power-inefficient compute operators and vendors with no balance-sheet flexibility.
- Valuation / market expectations:
  - Consensus assumes demand stays strong but underestimates capex-to-cash lag and delivery bottlenecks.
- Recommended exposure framework:
  - Core: cash-generative infrastructure enablers.
  - Satellite: event-driven compute names with strong power procurement optionality.

## C. The Story
The first phase of AI monetization was framed as a pure compute race. That framing is now incomplete. The binding constraint in the next leg is power quality and availability: generation, transmission, and cooling. When physical bottlenecks become binding, margin pools migrate away from headline winners toward the chokepoints that determine uptime.

Two to three years ago, investors could underwrite this theme mostly through chip-unit demand and cloud growth rates. Today, delivery risk is increasingly set by grid interconnection lead times, local permitting constraints, and facility-level thermal limits. The narrative has shifted from “how many accelerators?” to “how many megawatts can be deployed on schedule?”

Consensus still treats infrastructure spend as supporting cast and values many participants on top-line narratives. Our differentiated view is that returns will concentrate where incremental dollars convert to reliable capacity and where pricing power persists through procurement cycles.

## D. Mechanics of the Theme
- Value chain: generation and transmission assets capture scarcity rents first; thermal and power electronics capture second-order rents.
- Operating leverage vs capital intensity: software-like gross margin narratives are less relevant where deployment speed is constrained by physical assets.
- Macro sensitivity: rates and policy influence financing cost, but queue length and permitting cadence dominate near-term realized outcomes.

## F. Winners, Losers, and Second-Order Effects
- Direct beneficiaries: transmission equipment, grid automation, thermal-control vendors.
- Indirect beneficiaries: industrial services, specialty materials, financing platforms for infrastructure rollout.
- Disrupted incumbents: operators with weak power access and inflexible data center designs.
- Second-order effects: regional power markets and utility capex cycles become equity-selection inputs.

## G. Capital Allocation Playbook
- Public equities: core in infrastructure enablers; satellite in select compute operators with secured power pipelines.
- Private markets: focus on permitting-advantaged projects and control points in interconnect ecosystems.
- Infrastructure: own contracted cash-flow assets with upgrade pathways.
- Optionality trades: event-driven structures around regulatory and procurement milestones.
- Avoid: names where valuation assumes frictionless capacity deployment.

## H. Risks & Falsifiers
1. Interconnection timelines materially improve faster than expected.
2. Compute efficiency gains reduce power intensity per workload faster than projected.
3. Policy changes reduce permitting friction across key regions.
4. Capital markets shut for infrastructure-heavy balance sheets.
5. End-demand softness coincides with overbuild.
